## Summary

Flagship 06.05 — **STOMP, parallel noisy-rollout trajectory optimization** ("born for GPU", per
the catalog) — is done, opening worker batch 1b. A 2-D point robot's 64-waypoint trajectory is
optimized through a synthetic obstacle field: 1,024 smooth-noise rollouts scored per iteration on
the GPU, per-waypoint softmin blending on the host, convergence at iteration 16 with the final
path fully clear of every obstacle halo (591 → 0.001 total cost). The project is deliberately
paired with 08.01 MPPI: same rollout-scoring GPU pattern, but STOMP's **smooth noise** (drawn
through the inverse finite-difference smoothness matrix R⁻¹) and **per-waypoint** (not
per-trajectory) softmin update are the differences that make it a *planner* rather than a
controller — taught side-by-side in THEORY.md's comparison table.

## New projects (didactic blurbs)

**06.05 — STOMP** (★ beginner, domain 06, flagship). Teaches gradient-free trajectory
optimization: why white-noise perturbation of a trajectory fails (jagged, always colliding
somewhere) and how STOMP's structured noise — sampled from the smoothness metric itself — explores
only within the space of smooth paths. GPU pattern: one thread per noisy trajectory (08.01's
rollout idiom, transposed noise layout and all), with per-waypoint cost decomposition feeding the
paper's per-timestep update. The single most interesting thing to look at:
`demo/out/costfield.pgm` — the optimized path burned into the inflated obstacle field, curving
smoothly around the halos.

## What to study here

Read 08.01 MPPI first, then this project's `THEORY.md` §The math (the R-matrix smooth-noise
derivation — the heart of STOMP) and the MPPI-vs-STOMP table. Then `src/kernels.cu`. First
exercise: replace the smooth noise with white noise of the same variance and watch the optimizer
stop converging — the fastest way to *feel* why the covariance structure matters.
